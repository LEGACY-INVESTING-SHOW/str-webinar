## Video 4: "The Property Play Your CPA Isn't Telling You About"

---

If you're making good money right now and paying a lot of it in taxes, pay attention to this.

Most people talk about Airbnb as a way to make money. I want to talk about it as a way to keep money — the money your CPA is letting the IRS walk away with every April. That's the slow path. Your CPA files your return. They don't strategize your return. There's a difference, and it's costing you tens of thousands a year.

Built into short-term rental ownership is a strategy that can wipe tens of thousands off your tax bill every year. Most people — including most CPAs — have no idea it exists.

Quick note before I get into it — I'm not a CPA and this isn't tax advice. But this is a strategy our CPA partners run for clients every week. Let me show you how it works.

When you buy a property and run it as a short-term rental, three things happen at the same time. Three things that don't happen together in any other vehicle I've found.

First, cash flow. Your guests book nightly at premium rates. After mortgage, expenses, and operating costs, a single property can generate $2,000 to $3,000 a month in net cash flow. That's $24,000 to $36,000 a year from one unit. And that's conservative.

Second, appreciation. Every month your guests are paying your mortgage, your ownership stake grows. The property itself appreciates over time. You're not just collecting income. You're building an asset that compounds whether you're working or not.

But the third one — this is what changes everything. The tax savings. And this is only available through ownership. You cannot get this with the Bridge Method. You cannot get this from a traditional long-term rental. This only works when you own a short-term rental.

Here's why. The IRS treats short-term rentals with an average guest stay under seven days as an active business, not a passive investment. That means if your property shows a paper loss from depreciation — and it almost always will — you can deduct that loss directly against your W-2 income. Not against rental income. Against the money you earn at your job.

Now pair that with cost segregation and bonus depreciation. Thanks to the One Big Beautiful Bill Act signed in July 2025, bonus depreciation is back at 100%. That means when you buy a property and run a cost segregation study, you can identify $100,000 to $175,000 in short-life assets — furniture, fixtures, flooring, appliances — and write off the entire amount in year one.

So let me put real numbers on this. Buy a property for $500,000. A cost segregation study identifies roughly $100,000 to $140,000 in short-life assets eligible for bonus depreciation. Add in operating expenses and standard depreciation, and you're looking at a year-one paper loss that can offset a massive chunk of your W-2 income.

If you're making $200,000 a year and you deduct that loss, depending on your state and your bracket, you could save $40,000 to $55,000 in total taxes — federal and state combined — in a single year. That's not a typo. I'm going to run the exact calculator at the workshop so you can see it yourself.

Put it all together. One year. Cash flow, roughly $23,000. Equity buildup, about $5,700. Appreciation at 3%, $15,000. Tax savings, up to $55,000. That's nearly $100,000 in total year-one benefit on a roughly $140,000 investment.

If you're a high earner paying six figures in taxes every year — and you're not using this strategy — you're leaving more money on the table than most people make in a year.
